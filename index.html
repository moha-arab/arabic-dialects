HOW TO READ THIS ATLAS

كَيفَ تَقرَأُ الأطلَس

Three letters do most of the work in distinguishing Arabic dialects: qāf, jīm, and the treatment of short vowels. Once you can hear them, the whole continuum opens up.

ق
QĀF · UVULAR

The Northern Split

  • ʔglottal stop — Cairo, Damascus, Beirut, Jerusalem, Alexandria. Post-Arabization urban centres.
  • qpreserved — Druze, Alawite, Tunisian, Mosuli, Tihami. Conservative pockets.
  • gvoiced velar — Bedouin everywhere, Najdi, Sudanese, Saʿidi, Gulf.
ج
JĪM · AFFRICATE

Soft, Hard, or Lost

  • ghard — Egyptian only in the Arab world. The Cairene shibboleth.
  • ʒFrench j — Levantine, Moroccan, North African urban speech.
  • dʒEnglish j — Gulf, Iraqi, Saudi, Yemeni highlands.
  • ypalatal glide — coastal Hadhrami, some Najdi villages.
ـَ
VOWELS · IMĀLA & ELISION

The East–West Divide

  • ā→ēimāla in northern Levantine — bēb for bāb ("door").
  • i,u→∅vowel deletion in Maghrebi — ktbt for katabtu.
  • fullfull vowels preserved in Yemeni and Hijazi.

ABOUT THIS ATLAS

METHODOLOGY

Every zone on these maps is a stylization, not a border. Real dialect boundaries are gradients — what linguists call isoglosses — bundles of pronunciations that fade across miles. Within a single village, a Christian neighbour, a Druze neighbour, and a Muslim neighbour may pronounce the same word three different ways.

What this atlas captures is the centre of gravity of each dialect: where its features cluster most clearly. Treat it as a guide to the music, not the score.

SOURCES

Country borders from Natural Earth (public domain, 50m). Linguistic data synthesised from Versteegh's The Arabic Language (Edinburgh, 2014); Behnstedt & Woidich's Arabische Dialektgeographie (Brill, 2005); Holes' Modern Arabic (Georgetown, 2004); Watson's Phonology and Morphology of Arabic (Oxford, 2002); Ingham's Najdi Arabic (Benjamins, 1994); Procházka's work on peripheral varieties.
